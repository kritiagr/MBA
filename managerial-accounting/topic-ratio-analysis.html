Ratio Analysis – Concepts and Interpretation

In Simple Language

Ratios condense financial statements into comparable metrics. Four families: Liquidity (short-term solvency) — Current Ratio (CA/CL, ideal ~2:1), Quick Ratio ((CA − Inventory)/CL, ideal ~1:1). Solvency / Leverage (long-term) — Debt-Equity (Debt/Equity), Interest Coverage (EBIT/Interest). Profitability — Gross Margin, Operating Margin, Net Margin, Return on Assets (PAT/Total Assets), Return on Equity (PAT/Equity). Efficiency / Activity — Inventory Turnover (COGS/Avg Inventory), Debtor Turnover (Sales/Avg Debtors), Asset Turnover. Use trend analysis (year-on-year) and cross-sectional (vs peers/industry). DuPont breaks ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier — combining profitability, efficiency and leverage.

Key Concepts

  • Four ratio families: Liquidity, Solvency, Profitability, Activity/Efficiency
  • Key ratios and formulas (see notes)
  • Standards: rule-of-thumb, industry average, time series

Quiz Practice Quiz

Q1. Quick Ratio excludes:
  • Cash
  • Inventory
  • Receivables
  • Short-term investments
Explanation: Inventory (and sometimes prepaid expenses) is removed for a stricter liquidity test.
Q2. Higher Debt-Equity Ratio indicates:
  • Less financial risk
  • More financial leverage and risk
  • Higher profitability
  • Lower liquidity
Explanation: More debt magnifies returns but also financial risk.
Q3. ROE = ?
  • PAT / Sales
  • PAT / Total Assets
  • PAT / Shareholders' Equity
  • EBIT / Capital Employed
Explanation: Return on Equity = Net Profit / Shareholders' Equity.
Q4. Inventory Turnover Ratio is calculated as:
  • Sales / Average Inventory
  • COGS / Average Inventory
  • COGS / Sales
  • Inventory / Sales
Explanation: Standard formula uses COGS, not sales, because inventory is at cost.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Define liquidity ratios.
Ratios that measure a firm's ability to meet short-term obligations from current resources; main examples are Current Ratio and Quick (Acid-Test) Ratio.
State the DuPont identity for ROE.
ROE = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier (Assets/Equity); it links profitability, efficiency and financial leverage.
List two limitations of ratio analysis.
Based on historical data so may not reflect current/future reality; firms may use different accounting policies (e.g. inventory methods, depreciation), reducing comparability; window-dressing can distort year-end positions.
Why is interest coverage important?
It tells how many times operating profit covers interest expense (EBIT/Interest), indicating the firm's ability to service debt without distress.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Ratio analysis converts the raw numbers in financial statements into meaningful performance indicators that can be compared across time and across firms. Ratios fall into four families. Liquidity ratios assess short-term solvency: the current ratio (current assets / current liabilities, often benchmarked near 2:1) and the quick (acid-test) ratio, which excludes inventory and prepaid expenses to apply a stricter test. Solvency or leverage ratios examine long-term financial structure: debt-equity (Debt/Equity) reveals how aggressively the firm is financed by borrowed funds; interest coverage (EBIT/Interest) reveals whether operating profit comfortably services that debt. Profitability ratios measure earning power at different levels — gross margin, operating margin and net margin from the income statement, and return ratios such as ROA (PAT/Total Assets) and ROE (PAT/Shareholders' Equity) that connect profit to the capital base. Activity or efficiency ratios — inventory turnover (COGS/Average Inventory), debtor turnover (Sales/Average Debtors), asset turnover — assess how productively assets are being deployed. Three standards guide interpretation: rule-of-thumb benchmarks, trend analysis comparing the firm with its own past, and cross-sectional comparison against peers and industry averages. The DuPont identity beautifully ties profitability, efficiency and leverage together: ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier — diagnosing whether weak ROE stems from thin margins, slow asset use or insufficient leverage. Ratios are powerful but not infallible: they rely on historical data, can be distorted by differing accounting policies and are vulnerable to year-end window-dressing — so they must be combined with qualitative judgement and contextual analysis.
