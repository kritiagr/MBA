Accounting Mechanics & Transaction Flow

In Simple Language

Every transaction is captured using the dual-entry system, which respects the accounting equation Assets = Liabilities + Equity at all times. There are five account categories: Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Income and Expenses. Debit and credit rules: Assets and Expenses increase with a Debit; Liabilities, Equity and Income increase with a Credit (mnemonic DEAD-CLIC). The transaction flow: source document → Journal (book of original entry, chronological) → Ledger (account-wise summary, T-accounts) → Trial Balance (debit total must equal credit total) → Adjusting entries → Financial Statements → Closing entries. The dual-entry discipline guarantees that the books always balance and that errors of omission or commission can be traced.

Key Concepts

  • Five categories of accounts: Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Income, Expenses
  • Accounting equation: A = L + E (and expanded form with revenues − expenses)
  • Dual aspect / double-entry principle
  • Debit/Credit rules (DEAD-CLIC mnemonic)
  • Journal → Ledger → Trial Balance → Financial Statements
  • Adjusting entries: accruals, deferrals, depreciation
  • Closing entries and the post-closing trial balance

Quiz Practice Quiz

Q1. The accounting equation is:
  • Assets + Liabilities = Equity
  • Assets = Liabilities + Equity
  • Revenue − Expenses = Liabilities
  • Assets = Revenue − Expenses
Explanation: The fundamental identity underlying dual entry.
Q2. Buying inventory on credit:
  • Increases assets and equity
  • Increases assets and liabilities
  • Decreases assets and increases equity
  • No effect
Explanation: Inventory ↑ (asset) and Trade Payables ↑ (liability).
Q3. Salary paid in cash will be:
  • Debit Salary, Credit Cash
  • Debit Cash, Credit Salary
  • Debit Cash, Credit Capital
  • Debit Salary, Credit Capital
Explanation: Expense ↑ (debit), Cash ↓ (credit).
Q4. Trial balance ensures:
  • No errors at all
  • Equality of total debits and credits
  • Profit is correct
  • All transactions recorded
Explanation: Only arithmetic equality; compensating errors can still exist.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

State the rules of debit and credit (DEAD-CLIC).
Debit increases: Expenses, Assets, Drawings. Credit increases: Liabilities, Income (Revenue), Capital. Reverse for decreases.
What is a Journal?
The book of original entry where every transaction is recorded chronologically with debit and credit, the date, and a narration explaining the transaction.
Why prepare a Trial Balance?
To verify the arithmetic accuracy of postings — total debits should equal total credits — and to serve as the basis for preparing financial statements.
Give an example of an adjusting entry.
Depreciation: Debit Depreciation Expense, Credit Accumulated Depreciation — allocates the cost of a long-term asset to the period in which it was used.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Accounting mechanics implement the dual-entry system, the engine that keeps every transaction internally consistent. Every economic event affects at least two accounts so that the accounting equation Assets = Liabilities + Equity always holds; equity itself expands to Capital + Revenues − Expenses − Drawings, showing how profitability flows into ownership. Accounts fall into five categories — Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Income and Expenses — each governed by specific debit-credit rules summarised by the mnemonic DEAD-CLIC: Debits increase Expenses, Assets and Drawings; Credits increase Liabilities, Income and Capital. The transaction flow starts with source documents like invoices and receipts, then journal entries record the event chronologically with debit, credit and a narration. Posting transfers each entry to the relevant ledger account (T-account), summarising activity by account. At period end a trial balance lists every ledger balance to confirm total debits equal total credits — a necessary but not sufficient check for accuracy, since compensating errors can slip through. Adjusting entries then bring the books to accrual basis by recording accruals (revenues earned/expenses incurred but not yet recorded), deferrals (prepaid expenses, unearned revenue), and depreciation. From this adjusted trial balance the financial statements — income statement, balance sheet and cash flow statement — are prepared, and finally closing entries reset temporary accounts (revenues, expenses, drawings) to zero, transferring net profit to retained earnings ready for the next period.
