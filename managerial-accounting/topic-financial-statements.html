The Three Financial Statements

In Simple Language

Three statements together tell the financial story: the Income Statement (P&L) shows revenues, expenses and the resulting profit over a period. The Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position) is a snapshot at a date — Assets = Liabilities + Equity. The Cash Flow Statement explains how cash moved during the period in three buckets: Operating (day-to-day business), Investing (buying/selling long-term assets) and Financing (raising/repaying capital). Profit ≠ Cash because of accruals and non-cash items like depreciation. Reading the three together gives a holistic picture of performance, position and liquidity.

Key Concepts

  • Income Statement: revenue, COGS, gross profit, operating profit (EBIT), PAT
  • Balance Sheet: current vs non-current; assets, liabilities, equity
  • Cash Flow Statement: Operating + Investing + Financing = Net change in cash
  • Direct vs Indirect method for CFO
  • Why profit ≠ cash: accruals, depreciation, working-capital changes
  • Interconnection: Net profit → Retained earnings; Closing cash → Balance sheet

  • Operating

  • Financing

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Difference between gross profit and operating profit.
Gross profit = Revenue − COGS (manufacturing margin). Operating profit = Gross profit − Operating expenses (after SG&A) but before interest and tax.
List the three sections of the Cash Flow Statement.
Cash from Operating activities (CFO), Cash from Investing activities (CFI), and Cash from Financing activities (CFF). Their sum equals the net change in cash for the period.
Why profit and cash can differ in a period.
Profit follows the accrual principle (revenue earned, expenses incurred) and includes non-cash items like depreciation; cash is recorded only when received or paid, and is affected by working-capital movements.
How are the three statements interconnected?
Net profit from the income statement flows into retained earnings on the balance sheet; depreciation and working-capital changes link the income statement to the cash flow statement; closing cash on the CFS equals the cash balance on the BS.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

The three primary financial statements together describe an organisation's performance, position and liquidity. The Income Statement (Profit and Loss Account) reports revenues earned and expenses incurred over a defined period, stepping from revenue to gross profit (after cost of goods sold), operating profit or EBIT (after operating expenses), profit before tax (after interest) and finally profit after tax (PAT). It answers the question 'did the business make money in this period?'. The Balance Sheet, or Statement of Financial Position, is a snapshot at a point in time and embodies the accounting equation Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Assets are arranged current and non-current; liabilities similarly; equity reflects owners' claims including paid-up capital and accumulated retained earnings. The Cash Flow Statement explains the change in cash and cash equivalents over the period through three sections — Cash from Operating activities (CFO) records day-to-day cash generation; Cash from Investing activities (CFI) captures purchases and sales of long-term assets and investments; Cash from Financing activities (CFF) shows movements with lenders and equity holders. Profit and cash routinely diverge because of accrual accounting, non-cash expenses such as depreciation, and working-capital changes; the indirect-method CFO reconciles these by starting from net profit and adjusting for non-cash items and working-capital movements. The three statements interlock: net profit flows into retained earnings on the balance sheet; depreciation appears in both the P&L and as an add-back in CFO; closing cash on the CFS must equal the cash balance on the BS. Read together, they reveal whether profits are translating to cash, whether the firm is investing for growth or harvesting, and whether financing is supporting or eroding value.
