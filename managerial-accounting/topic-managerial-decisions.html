Managerial Decisions Using Cost Information

In Simple Language

Short-term decisions rely on relevant costs — future costs that differ between alternatives. Common decisions include: (1) Make-or-Buy: compare incremental cost of making vs purchase price plus opportunity cost of freed capacity. (2) Special-Order acceptance: accept if price covers variable cost and any additional fixed costs, provided regular business is not harmed. (3) Drop or continue a product/segment: keep if it yields a positive contribution after avoidable fixed costs. (4) Sell-or-Process-further: process only if incremental revenue exceeds incremental cost. (5) Pricing: cost-plus, target-return, value-based, contribution-based. Sunk costs and allocated common fixed costs are irrelevant; opportunity costs must be included.

Key Concepts

  • Relevant cost: future, differential, avoidable
  • Irrelevant costs: sunk, allocated common fixed
  • Make-or-Buy decision: incremental analysis with opportunity cost
  • Special-order acceptance criteria
  • Drop-or-continue analysis (avoidable vs unavoidable fixed)
  • Sell-or-process-further: incremental revenue vs incremental cost
  • Pricing methods: cost-plus, target-return, contribution, value-based

Quiz Practice Quiz

Q1. Which cost is irrelevant to a make-or-buy decision?
  • Variable manufacturing cost
  • Sunk cost of existing equipment
  • Opportunity cost of freed capacity
  • Purchase price from supplier
Explanation: Sunk costs are already incurred and cannot change with the decision.
Q2. A special order should generally be accepted if:
  • Price > Total Cost
  • Price > Variable Cost (+ any extra FC)
  • Price > Full Cost + Markup
  • Idle capacity does not exist
Explanation: When capacity is idle, any price above incremental cost adds to profit.
Q3. An opportunity cost is:
  • A historical cost
  • The benefit of the next-best alternative forgone
  • A fixed cost
  • A book entry
Explanation: Opportunity costs are real and relevant though not always recorded in books.
Q4. A loss-making segment should be dropped only if:
  • It shows a net loss after allocations
  • Its avoidable costs exceed its revenues (i.e. it has negative contribution after avoidable FC)
  • It has high variable cost
  • Sales are below average
Explanation: Allocated common fixed costs would remain even after dropping; focus on avoidable costs.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Define relevant cost.
A cost that is future-oriented and differs between alternatives under consideration; only relevant costs (and opportunity costs) should influence a short-term decision.
When is sunk cost relevant?
Never — sunk costs are already incurred and cannot be changed by any future decision; including them leads to poor analysis.
State the decision rule for make-or-buy.
Choose the option with lower total incremental cost, including the opportunity cost of any freed capacity if 'buy' releases internal resources for other profitable uses.
Why might a firm accept a special order below full cost?
If the order's price exceeds incremental (variable + additional fixed) cost and there is idle capacity without harming the regular market, the order still adds to profit.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Cost-based managerial decisions are short-term in nature and hinge on the concept of relevant cost: any future cost that differs between the alternatives under consideration. Sunk costs, already-incurred and unchangeable, are irrelevant; allocated common fixed costs usually remain whatever the decision and are also irrelevant; opportunity costs — the benefits sacrificed by choosing one course of action over another — are very much relevant even though books rarely record them. In a make-or-buy decision the relevant comparison is the incremental cost of internal production (variable cost plus any directly avoidable fixed costs and the opportunity cost of using the capacity) against the supplier's price; the cheaper option wins, qualified by quality, reliability and strategic considerations. A special-order decision asks whether to accept a one-off discounted order: when spare capacity exists, accept if the price exceeds incremental cost, taking care not to cannibalise full-price sales. The drop-or-continue analysis for a product or segment focuses on avoidable fixed costs: discontinue only if the segment's contribution after deducting all avoidable costs is negative, since unavoidable allocated fixed costs would persist. The sell-or-process-further question (common in joint-product industries) ignores joint costs already incurred and compares incremental revenue from further processing with the incremental cost of that processing — process further only if the former exceeds the latter. Pricing decisions then combine these cost insights with market-facing approaches such as cost-plus pricing, target-return pricing, contribution-based pricing for surplus-capacity sales and value-based pricing aligned with customer-perceived benefit. Rigorous use of relevant-cost thinking is the single biggest lever managers have to avoid emotionally or historically anchored — and ultimately costly — choices.
