Inventory Valuation & Budgeting

In Simple Language

Inventory valuation choices affect both the Balance Sheet (closing stock) and the Income Statement (COGS, profit). FIFO (First-In-First-Out) assumes oldest stock sold first — in rising-price periods it gives higher profit and higher closing inventory. LIFO (Last-In-First-Out, not permitted under Ind AS/IFRS but allowed in US GAAP) gives lower profit and lower inventory in rising prices. Weighted Average smooths costs across periods. Budgeting is the quantitative expression of a plan. Master budget combines operating budgets (sales, production, materials, labour, overhead, cost-of-goods-sold, SG&A, P&L) and financial budgets (cash, capex, balance sheet). Zero-Based Budgeting (ZBB) rebuilds every budget from scratch each year; Incremental adjusts last year's by a percentage. Variance analysis compares actual to budget — favourable (F) if actual revenue > budget or actual cost < budget, otherwise adverse (A).

Key Concepts

  • Inventory valuation: FIFO, LIFO, Weighted Average
  • Impact on COGS, profit, taxes and balance-sheet inventory
  • Master Budget components: operating + financial
  • Cash budget, capital budget, flexible budget
  • Zero-Based Budgeting vs Incremental Budgeting
  • Variance analysis: price/usage, rate/efficiency; favourable vs adverse

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Compare FIFO and Weighted Average.
FIFO assigns the earliest costs to COGS and the latest costs to closing inventory; in inflation it inflates profit. Weighted Average computes an average cost per unit and uses it for both COGS and inventory, smoothing fluctuations.
List the components of a Master Budget.
Operating budgets (sales, production, direct material, direct labour, overhead, COGS, SG&A, budgeted P&L) and financial budgets (cash budget, capital expenditure budget, budgeted balance sheet).
Define Zero-Based Budgeting.
A budgeting approach where every activity must be justified from a zero base each period rather than being incrementally adjusted; allocates resources based on need and value, not history.
Why is variance analysis important?
It pinpoints where and why actual results deviated from plan, enabling timely corrective action and learning for future budgets.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Inventory valuation and budgeting are central tools of management accounting. Inventory valuation matters because the cost flow assumption affects both the closing stock on the balance sheet and the cost of goods sold on the income statement, and therefore profit and tax. First-In, First-Out (FIFO) assumes the oldest units are sold first, so closing inventory is valued at the most recent (usually higher) costs; in inflationary periods this produces a higher reported profit. Last-In, First-Out (LIFO) does the opposite, charging the newest costs to COGS and leaving the oldest in inventory; it lowers reported profit and tax in inflation, but is prohibited under IFRS and Ind AS (still permitted under US GAAP). Weighted Average computes a moving average cost per unit and applies it to both COGS and closing inventory, smoothing the effect of price fluctuations. Budgeting is the quantitative expression of management's plan. The master budget comprises operating budgets — sales, production, direct material, direct labour, overhead, COGS, SG&A and the budgeted income statement — and financial budgets — the cash budget, the capital expenditure budget and the budgeted balance sheet. Different philosophies exist: Incremental Budgeting adjusts last year's figures by an inflation or growth rate, while Zero-Based Budgeting forces every activity to be re-justified from a zero base each cycle, ensuring resources flow to genuinely value-creating activities. Flexible budgets adjust for actual volume so that variance analysis isolates rate/efficiency from volume effects. Variance analysis compares actuals with the (flexed) budget for revenues, materials (price and usage), labour (rate and efficiency) and overhead (spending and volume); a variance is labelled favourable (F) when it improves profit and adverse (A) when it worsens it. Together, inventory choices and disciplined budgeting drive operational control and strategic execution.
