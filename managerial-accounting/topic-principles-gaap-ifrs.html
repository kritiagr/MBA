Accounting Principles & Financial Reporting Framework

In Simple Language

Accounting principles are the assumptions, conventions and rules that make financial statements credible and comparable. Fundamental assumptions: business entity (business separate from owner), going concern (firm will continue), money measurement (only money-measurable events), accounting period (life chopped into periods), accrual (record when earned/incurred, not when cash moves), consistency, prudence/conservatism, materiality, matching and full disclosure. Two big frameworks govern reporting globally: US GAAP and IFRS. IFRS is principles-based, used in 140+ countries; US GAAP is rules-based. Indian Accounting Standards (Ind AS) are largely converged with IFRS. Key IFRS standards include IFRS 15 (revenue), IAS 2 (inventory), IAS 16 (PPE), IFRS 5 (held-for-sale) and IFRS S1/S2 (sustainability).

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Define the Business Entity Concept.
The business is treated as an entity separate and distinct from its owners and other businesses; only transactions of the entity are recorded in its books.
Why is the Going Concern Concept important?
It allows non-current assets to be carried at cost and depreciated over their useful lives, and supports the deferral of unearned revenue/prepaid expenses; without it, all items would be at liquidation value.
Difference between Accrual and Cash basis.
Accrual recognises income when earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of cash. Cash basis recognises only when cash is received/paid; simpler but less accurate for performance measurement.
What is the Prudence (Conservatism) principle?
When uncertain, recognise probable losses immediately but defer probable gains until realised — to avoid overstating income or assets.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Accounting principles are the conceptual foundations that ensure financial statements are reliable, comparable and decision-useful. They begin with four fundamental assumptions. The Business Entity assumption treats the business as a unit distinct from its owners, so personal expenses never enter the firm's books. The Going Concern assumption presumes the business will continue operating in the foreseeable future, justifying historical-cost accounting, depreciation over useful life, and the deferral of prepaid expenses. The Money Measurement assumption requires every recorded transaction to be expressible in monetary terms, while the Accounting Period assumption divides the life of an enterprise into discrete periods (monthly, quarterly, annual) so performance can be evaluated periodically. Layered on top are conventions: the Accrual concept records revenue when earned and expenses when incurred — independent of cash timing — and pairs naturally with the Matching principle, which insists that expenses be recognised in the same period as the revenues they helped generate. Consistency demands the same methods over time; Prudence biases toward early recognition of losses and late recognition of gains; Materiality permits departure from strict rules for trivial amounts; Full Disclosure requires reporting all material information needed by users. Globally two frameworks coexist: US GAAP is rules-based and litigation-driven, while IFRS — used in 140+ countries and largely converged with India's Ind AS — is principles-based, covering revenue (IFRS 15), inventory (IAS 2), PPE (IAS 16), held-for-sale (IFRS 5) and the new sustainability standards IFRS S1 and S2. A notable point of divergence is R&D: US GAAP expenses all R&D, whereas IFRS capitalises development expenditure that meets specific criteria, producing very different balance sheets for the same economic activity.
