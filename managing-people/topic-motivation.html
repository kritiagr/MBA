Motivation Theories

In Simple Language

Motivation is the process that energises, directs and sustains effort toward a goal. Content theories explain WHAT motivates: Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (physiological → safety → social → esteem → self-actualization); Alderfer's ERG (Existence, Relatedness, Growth) compresses Maslow into three; Herzberg's Two-Factor theory distinguishes Hygiene factors (pay, policy, supervision — prevent dissatisfaction) from Motivators (achievement, recognition, work itself — produce satisfaction); McClelland's Three Needs are Achievement, Power and Affiliation. Process theories explain HOW motivation works: Vroom's Expectancy Theory says Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence; Equity Theory (Adams) says people compare their input-outcome ratio with referents; Goal-Setting (Locke) says SMART goals raise performance; Reinforcement Theory uses operant conditioning. Job Design approaches — Job Rotation, Enlargement and Enrichment — apply these theories practically.

Key Concepts

  • Maslow's Hierarchy (5 levels) and assumptions
  • ERG Theory (Alderfer): frustration-regression
  • Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory: Hygiene vs Motivators
  • McClelland: Need for Achievement (nAch), Power (nPow), Affiliation (nAff)
  • Vroom's Expectancy: M = E × I × V
  • Adams' Equity Theory and referent comparisons
  • Locke's Goal-Setting (SMART, feedback, commitment)
  • Reinforcement Theory: positive/negative reinforcement, punishment, extinction
  • Job design: rotation, enlargement, enrichment (vertical loading)

Quiz Practice Quiz

Q1. Hygiene factors in Herzberg's theory:
  • Always motivate
  • Prevent dissatisfaction but don't motivate
  • Are physiological needs
  • Same as Maslow's esteem
Explanation: Their absence causes dissatisfaction; their presence yields no positive motivation.
Q2. Vroom's Expectancy Theory: motivation =
  • E + I + V
  • E × I × V
  • E − I + V
  • (E + V) / I
Explanation: Multiplicative: if any term is zero, motivation is zero.
Q3. Need for Achievement (nAch) people prefer:
  • Easy tasks
  • Tasks of moderate difficulty with feedback
  • Group tasks
  • Repetitive tasks
Explanation: McClelland found nAch individuals choose moderately challenging, achievable goals.
Q4. Job Enrichment differs from Job Enlargement by:
  • Adding more similar tasks
  • Adding decision-making authority (vertical loading)
  • Pay rise
  • Job change
Explanation: Enrichment increases responsibility, autonomy and control; enlargement adds tasks at the same level (horizontal).

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Differentiate Job Rotation and Job Enrichment.
Job Rotation moves an employee laterally between different jobs at similar levels to broaden skills (e.g. a bank teller rotating to loan processing). Job Enrichment vertically loads a job with greater responsibility, autonomy and decision authority (e.g. a marketing assistant given authority over campaign strategy).
State Vroom's Expectancy Theory.
Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence. Expectancy = will my effort produce performance? Instrumentality = will performance lead to reward? Valence = how much do I value the reward? If any element is weak, motivation falters.
List Maslow's five needs in order.
1. Physiological (food, water) 2. Safety (security) 3. Social/Belongingness (friendship, love) 4. Esteem (recognition, status) 5. Self-Actualization (realising potential).
Define equity theory in one line.
Adams' Equity Theory: people compare their input-outcome ratio with that of referent others and are motivated to restore fairness when they perceive inequity.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Motivation is the set of forces that energise, direct and sustain behaviour towards a goal. OB scholars classify motivation theories into content theories — which identify what people want — and process theories — which explain how they weigh and pursue those wants. Maslow's Hierarchy proposes five ascending levels of need (physiological, safety, social, esteem, self-actualization) that must be largely satisfied at each level before the next becomes a motivator. Alderfer's ERG model compresses these into Existence, Relatedness and Growth and adds a frustration-regression element. Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory makes a crucial managerial distinction: hygiene factors such as pay, policy, working conditions and supervision only prevent dissatisfaction, while genuine motivators (achievement, recognition, the work itself, responsibility, advancement) drive satisfaction and performance. McClelland's theory of acquired needs — for Achievement, Power and Affiliation — explains differences in goal preference and career success. Process theories sharpen the picture. Vroom's Expectancy Theory frames motivation as the product Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence: will effort yield performance, will performance yield reward, and is the reward valued? If any term is weak, motivation collapses. Adams' Equity Theory argues that people compare their input-outcome ratio with referent others and act to restore fairness when they perceive inequity — through more effort, less effort, exit or cognitive distortion. Locke's Goal-Setting Theory shows that specific, challenging, accepted goals with feedback consistently outperform vague 'do your best' targets. Reinforcement Theory uses Skinner's operant conditioning — positive reinforcement, negative reinforcement, punishment and extinction — to shape behaviour. Job design strategies of rotation, enlargement and especially vertical-loading enrichment, plus modern variants like the Job Characteristics Model (skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, feedback → motivation), translate these theories into concrete workplace interventions.
