Introduction to OB & Managerial Roles

In Simple Language

Organizational Behavior (OB) is the systematic study of how individuals and groups act within organizations, with the goal of applying this knowledge to improve effectiveness. It draws on psychology, sociology, social psychology, anthropology and political science. OB operates at three levels — individual (personality, perception, motivation), group (teams, leadership, communication, conflict) and organizational (culture, structure, change). Henry Mintzberg classified managerial roles into three categories: Interpersonal (Figurehead, Leader, Liaison), Informational (Monitor, Disseminator, Spokesperson) and Decisional (Entrepreneur, Disturbance Handler, Resource Allocator, Negotiator). Katz identified three essential managerial skills: Technical, Human and Conceptual; the mix shifts as a manager rises through the hierarchy.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Organizational Behavior (OB) is the systematic study of how people — as individuals and within groups — act in organizational settings, and the application of that knowledge to make organizations work better. OB is interdisciplinary, drawing principally from psychology (individual behaviour, motivation, perception, learning), sociology (group dynamics, formal organizations), social psychology (influence, attitude change), anthropology (organizational culture) and political science (power, politics, conflict). It analyses behaviour at three interconnected levels: the individual (personality, perception, attitudes, values, motivation), the group (teams, leadership, communication, conflict, power) and the organization (structure, culture, change). Studying OB systematically — through evidence and not intuition — yields better decisions about hiring, motivating, leading and developing employees. Henry Mintzberg's empirical classification of managerial roles remains foundational: ten roles bundled into three categories. Interpersonal roles (Figurehead, Leader, Liaison) involve people and relationships. Informational roles (Monitor, Disseminator, Spokesperson) involve gathering and sharing information. Decisional roles (Entrepreneur, Disturbance Handler, Resource Allocator, Negotiator) involve making choices. Robert Katz complemented this with the idea that all managers need three skills — Technical, Human and Conceptual — though the mix shifts with level: front-line managers need more technical, top managers more conceptual, while human skills matter equally throughout. Together, OB and these classic frameworks give managers a vocabulary and toolkit for diagnosing problems and designing interventions in their organizations.
