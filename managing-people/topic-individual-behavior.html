Individual Behavior: Personality, Perception, Attitudes & Values

In Simple Language

Individual behavior is shaped by personality, perception, attitudes and values. Personality is the relatively stable pattern of how a person thinks, feels and behaves; the Big Five model (OCEAN — Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism) and the MBTI are widely used. Perception is how we select, organise and interpret stimuli; perceptual shortcuts include the Halo Effect, Stereotyping, Contrast Effect, Projection and Selective Perception — all of which can bias judgements like performance appraisals. Attitudes are evaluations of objects/people with cognitive, affective and behavioural components; job-related attitudes include job satisfaction, job involvement and organizational commitment. Values are enduring beliefs about what is right; they shape attitudes, motivation and ethical behaviour.

  • Big Five personality traits (OCEAN); MBTI
  • Locus of control (internal/external); self-efficacy; self-esteem
  • Type A vs Type B personality

Quiz Practice Quiz

Q1. Rating an employee 'excellent' overall because she is cheerful is an example of:
  • Halo Effect
  • Projection
  • Contrast Effect
  • Selective Perception
Explanation: Halo Effect: one positive trait colours the overall evaluation.
Q2. Big Five does NOT include:
  • Openness
  • Conscientiousness
  • Locus of control
  • Neuroticism
Explanation: Locus of control is a separate construct, not one of the Big Five.
Q3. Cognitive Dissonance refers to:
  • Conflict with co-workers
  • Inconsistency between attitudes/behaviours
  • Lack of training
  • Personality clash
Explanation: Festinger's theory: people experience tension when attitudes and actions misalign.
Q4. An internal locus of control means a person believes:
  • Outcomes are due to luck
  • They control their own outcomes
  • Group decides
  • External forces dominate
Explanation: Internals believe their behaviour drives outcomes; externals blame luck or fate.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Define personality.
Personality is the sum total of ways an individual reacts to and interacts with others; relatively stable patterns of thought, emotion and behaviour.
Name the Big Five traits.
Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism (OCEAN). Conscientiousness is the strongest predictor of job performance across roles.
Explain the Halo Effect.
A perceptual bias where one favourable trait (e.g. attractiveness, friendliness) leads us to rate other unrelated traits also positively, distorting evaluations like appraisals.
Three components of an attitude.
Cognitive (belief/thought), Affective (feeling), and Behavioural (intention to act). Misalignment among them produces cognitive dissonance.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Individual behavior in organizations is shaped by four powerful, intersecting drivers — personality, perception, attitudes and values. Personality is the relatively stable pattern of how a person thinks, feels and behaves, often captured by the Big Five model (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism), of which conscientiousness is the most consistent predictor of job performance. Other relevant traits include locus of control (belief that outcomes are within one's own control vs determined externally), self-efficacy (confidence in one's ability to succeed at a task), self-esteem and Type A/B distinctions. Perception is the process by which individuals select, organise and interpret sensory information to make sense of their environment. Because attention is limited, we rely on shortcuts — selective perception, halo and horn effects, stereotyping, contrast effects, projection, the fundamental attribution error — all of which can systematically distort decisions such as recruitment and performance appraisal. Attitudes are evaluative statements about objects, people or events and have three components: cognitive (beliefs), affective (feelings) and behavioural (intentions). Workplace attitudes such as job satisfaction, job involvement and organizational commitment predict turnover, citizenship and performance. When attitudes and behaviours misalign, Festinger's theory of cognitive dissonance predicts that individuals will adjust one to restore consistency. Values are the foundation beneath attitudes — enduring convictions about what is right or desirable, classified by Rokeach into terminal (desired end states) and instrumental (preferred modes of conduct). Understanding these constructs allows managers to select better, design fair appraisal systems, build engagement and align individual goals with organizational purpose.
