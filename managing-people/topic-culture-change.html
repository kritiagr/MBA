Organizational Culture & Change Management

In Simple Language

Organizational culture is the shared system of meaning — values, norms, rituals, language — that distinguishes one organization from another. Schein's three levels: Artefacts (visible — dress, logos, stories), Espoused Values (mission, strategy), Basic Underlying Assumptions (unconscious beliefs). Strong cultures provide clear identity and consistency but can resist needed change. Types: clan, adhocracy, market, hierarchy (Competing Values Framework). Cultures are created by founders, maintained through selection, socialisation and leadership behaviour. Organizational change is movement from current state to desired future state. Lewin's three-step model: Unfreeze → Change → Refreeze. Kotter's eight-step model: create urgency → build coalition → form vision → communicate → empower → quick wins → consolidate → institutionalise. Resistance to change comes from habit, security, economic factors, fear of unknown and selective processing; overcome through communication, participation, support, fairness, manipulation/coercion (last resort).

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Define organizational culture.
The shared system of values, beliefs, norms and assumptions that guide behaviour in an organization; the 'way we do things around here'.
List Schein's three levels of culture.
1. Artefacts (visible: dress code, logos, stories); 2. Espoused Values (mission, strategy, philosophy); 3. Basic Underlying Assumptions (unconscious beliefs that operate as the deepest layer).
State Lewin's three-step change model.
Unfreeze (create motivation to change by disconfirming the status quo), Change/Movement (implement new behaviours, learn new skills), Refreeze (stabilise the change so it becomes the new norm).
Why do people resist organizational change?
Habit, threats to security and pay, fear of the unknown, selective information processing, perceived loss of status or competence, and distrust of management. Overcome through communication, participation, support and fair process.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Organizational culture is the system of shared meanings — values, norms, language, rituals, stories — that distinguishes one organization from another and shapes how members think, feel and behave. Edgar Schein's three-level model is the classic reference: Artefacts are the visible manifestations such as dress code, office layout, logos, ceremonies and stories; Espoused Values are the publicly declared mission, strategy and philosophy; Basic Underlying Assumptions are the deepest, unconscious, taken-for-granted beliefs that operate as the cultural bedrock and are the hardest to surface and change. Strong cultures, with widely shared core values, provide clear identity, consistency in decision-making and high employee commitment, but they can also breed conformity and resist needed adaptation. The Competing Values Framework classifies cultures along two axes (flexibility vs stability, internal vs external focus) into four types: Clan (collaborative, family-like), Adhocracy (entrepreneurial, innovative), Market (competitive, results-oriented) and Hierarchy (controlled, structured). Cultures are created by founders, sustained through selection of culture-fit employees, top management behaviour and structured socialisation, and transmitted via stories, rituals, material symbols and language. Organizational change moves the system from a current state to a desired future state. Kurt Lewin's three-step model — Unfreeze (motivate change by disconfirming the status quo), Change (move to new behaviours through learning) and Refreeze (institutionalise) — is foundational. John Kotter's eight-step model extends it: establish urgency, build a guiding coalition, form a strategic vision, enlist a volunteer army, enable action by removing barriers, generate short-term wins, sustain acceleration and finally institute change in culture. Resistance comes from habit, security, economic loss, fear of the unknown, selective information processing and distrust; Kotter and Schlesinger suggest six tactics for overcoming it — education, participation, facilitation, negotiation, manipulation and coercion — with the first three preferred and the last reserved for speed-critical situations.
