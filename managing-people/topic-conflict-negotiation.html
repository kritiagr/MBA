Conflict Management & Negotiation

In Simple Language

Conflict is a process beginning when one party perceives another has frustrated or is about to frustrate its concerns. It can be functional (constructive, idea-generating) or dysfunctional (destructive). Types: task, relationship and process conflict. Thomas-Kilmann's five conflict-handling styles by assertiveness × cooperativeness: Competing (win-lose), Collaborating (win-win), Compromising (split the difference), Avoiding (lose-lose) and Accommodating (lose-win). Negotiation has two main approaches: Distributive bargaining (fixed pie, win-lose, focus on position) and Integrative bargaining (expand the pie, win-win, focus on interests). Key concepts include BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement), ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement) and Reservation Price. Effective negotiators prepare thoroughly, separate people from problem, focus on interests not positions, invent options for mutual gain, and insist on objective criteria (Fisher & Ury's 'Getting to Yes').

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Differentiate functional and dysfunctional conflict.
Functional conflict (usually task-related, moderate intensity) supports goals by stimulating ideas and critical evaluation. Dysfunctional conflict (relationship-based or extreme task) hinders performance, damages relationships, and lowers morale.
Define BATNA.
Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — your fallback option if no deal is reached. Knowing your BATNA tells you when to walk away and provides negotiating power.
Difference between distributive and integrative bargaining.
Distributive (win-lose) assumes a fixed pie and parties compete over shares (e.g. price haggling). Integrative (win-win) seeks to expand the pie by exploring underlying interests and trade-offs across multiple issues.
List the four principles of 'Getting to Yes'.
Separate the people from the problem; focus on interests, not positions; invent options for mutual gain; insist on using objective criteria.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Conflict is a natural feature of organizational life — a process that begins when one party perceives that another has frustrated or is about to frustrate something it cares about. Modern OB views conflict as neither inherently good nor bad: functional conflict, typically moderate task-related disagreement, stimulates fresh ideas, surfaces assumptions and improves decisions, while dysfunctional conflict — usually relationship-based or extreme — damages performance, trust and well-being. Conflict comes in three flavours: task (disagreement about content and goals), relationship (interpersonal friction) and process (disagreement about how work gets done). The Thomas-Kilmann model classifies five conflict-handling styles on the axes of assertiveness and cooperativeness: Competing (high assertive, low coop — win-lose), Collaborating (high-high — win-win), Compromising (moderate both — split the difference), Avoiding (low-low — postpone) and Accommodating (low assertive, high coop — yield). The right style depends on stakes, time and relationship importance. Negotiation, a structured form of conflict resolution, follows two broad approaches. Distributive bargaining treats the pie as fixed and frames the talks as win-lose, with anchoring, concession sequences and reservation prices driving outcomes. Integrative bargaining seeks to expand the pie by identifying each party's underlying interests, exploring trade-offs across multiple issues and inventing creative options for mutual gain. Three concepts are crucial: BATNA — the Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — sets the floor below which a negotiator should walk away; the Reservation Price is the worst acceptable outcome; the Zone of Possible Agreement (ZOPA) is the overlap between both parties' reservation prices. Fisher and Ury's classic 'Getting to Yes' advocates principled negotiation built on four pillars: separate people from problem, focus on interests not positions, generate options for mutual gain, and insist on objective criteria. When direct negotiation fails, third parties step in as mediators (facilitate), arbitrators (decide) or conciliators (informal go-between).
