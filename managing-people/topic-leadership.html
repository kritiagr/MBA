Leadership Theories & Styles

In Simple Language

Leadership is the ability to influence a group toward goals. Trait theories (early) tried to identify born-leader traits (intelligence, drive, integrity). Behavioural theories shifted focus to what leaders DO — University of Michigan (employee-oriented vs production-oriented), Ohio State (Consideration vs Initiating Structure) and the Managerial Grid (Blake & Mouton — Task vs People, 9,9 Team Leader is best). Contingency theories say no single style fits all: Fiedler matches Task vs Relationship style with situation favourableness; Hersey-Blanchard's Situational Leadership matches style (Telling, Selling, Participating, Delegating) to follower readiness. Modern theories include Transformational Leadership (inspires beyond self-interest through vision, charisma, intellectual stimulation, individualised consideration), Transactional (reward-based exchange), Servant Leadership and Authentic Leadership. Emotional Intelligence (Goleman) — self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship management — strongly predicts leadership effectiveness.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Differentiate Transactional and Transformational leadership.
Transactional leaders use contingent rewards and management by exception — performance for reward exchange. Transformational leaders inspire commitment to a vision through charisma, intellectual stimulation, and individualised consideration, leading to performance beyond expectations.
List Goleman's four EI competencies.
Self-Awareness, Self-Management, Social Awareness (empathy), and Relationship Management.
Name the five bases of power (French & Raven).
Legitimate (position), Reward (give incentives), Coercive (punish), Expert (knowledge), Referent (charisma/identification).
What is Servant Leadership?
A leadership philosophy where the leader's primary role is to serve followers, focusing on their growth, well-being and empowerment so they can perform at their best.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Leadership is the process of influencing a group toward the achievement of shared goals. Thinking about leadership has evolved through four broad eras. Trait theories sought stable, inborn qualities that distinguish leaders — intelligence, drive, self-confidence, integrity, extraversion — and although no single 'leader gene' was found, the Big Five (especially extraversion and conscientiousness) consistently predict leadership emergence and effectiveness. Behavioural theories shifted attention from traits to actions: the University of Michigan studies contrasted employee-oriented with production-oriented leaders; the Ohio State studies identified Consideration (people focus) and Initiating Structure (task focus) as two independent dimensions; Blake and Mouton's Managerial Grid mapped these on a 9 × 9 grid, with the 9,9 'Team Leader' style — high on both — held up as the ideal. Contingency theories recognise that the best style depends on the situation. Fiedler's model matches a leader's task- or relationship-orientation (LPC score) with situational favourableness; Hersey and Blanchard's Situational Leadership adjusts style — Telling, Selling, Participating or Delegating — to follower readiness; House's Path-Goal theory says leaders should clear the path to subordinates' goals using directive, supportive, participative or achievement-oriented behaviours as the situation warrants. Modern perspectives include Transactional leadership, which works through contingent rewards and management by exception, and Transformational leadership, which inspires followers to transcend self-interest through idealised influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation and individualised consideration — Bass's four I's. Servant Leadership puts followers' growth first, while Authentic Leadership emphasises integrity and self-awareness. Goleman's Emotional Intelligence — self-awareness, self-management, social awareness and relationship management — has emerged as a robust predictor of leadership effectiveness. Finally French and Raven's five bases of power — legitimate, reward, coercive, expert and referent — explain the resources leaders draw on to influence behaviour.
