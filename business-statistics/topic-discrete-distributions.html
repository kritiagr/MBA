Discrete Probability Distributions

In Simple Language

A discrete probability distribution lists each possible value of a discrete random variable X and its probability P(X=x), with the probabilities summing to 1. Expected value E(X) = Σ x·P(x) is the long-run mean; variance σ² = Σ (x − μ)²·P(x). Two workhorses are: Binomial — number of successes in n independent trials with constant success probability p; mean = np, variance = np(1−p). Poisson — number of events in a fixed interval at average rate λ; mean = variance = λ. Use binomial when n is fixed and we count successes; use Poisson when events arrive over time/space at a constant average rate.

Key Concepts

  • Discrete random variable; PMF rules (0 ≤ P(x) ≤ 1, ΣP(x) = 1)
  • Expected value, variance and standard deviation of a discrete RV
  • Binomial distribution: n, p; assumptions (fixed n, independent, constant p, 2 outcomes)
  • Mean = np, Variance = np(1−p)
  • Poisson distribution: λ events per unit; mean = variance = λ
  • Binomial → Poisson approximation when n large and p small

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

State the conditions for a Binomial distribution.
Fixed number of trials n; each trial independent; only two outcomes (success/failure); constant probability p of success on every trial.
Mean and variance of Binomial and Poisson.
Binomial(n, p): μ = np, σ² = np(1−p). Poisson(λ): μ = σ² = λ.
When can we approximate Binomial by Poisson?
When n is large (≥ 20) and p is small (≤ 0.05), with λ = np.
Give a business example of each.
Binomial: number of defective items in a batch of 50. Poisson: number of customer complaints received per day.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Discrete probability distributions describe random variables whose outcomes are countable. Their probability mass function (PMF) lists every possible value and the probability it occurs, subject to two rules — every probability lies between 0 and 1, and the probabilities sum to 1. From the PMF we derive the expected value E(X) = Σ x·P(x), which represents the long-run average outcome, and the variance σ² = Σ (x − μ)²·P(x), which measures how much outcomes typically deviate from the mean. The binomial distribution applies when we run n identical, independent trials, each with two outcomes (success/failure) and a constant probability p of success — for instance the number of defective items in a sample of 50. Its mean is np and variance is np(1−p). The Poisson distribution counts the number of events occurring in a fixed time or space when events happen independently at a constant average rate λ — for instance the number of calls a call-centre receives per hour. Uniquely, its mean and variance both equal λ. When n is large and p is small the binomial is well approximated by a Poisson with λ = np. These distributions underpin quality control charts, queuing models, reliability engineering and operational planning, letting managers translate uncertain counts into actionable probabilities and expected costs.
