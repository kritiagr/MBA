Confidence Interval Estimation

In Simple Language

A confidence interval (CI) is a range of plausible values for an unknown parameter, constructed so that, in repeated sampling, a stated percentage (the confidence level) of such intervals contain the true value. CI for the mean: x̄ ± t·s/√n with df = n − 1 (use Z if σ known). CI for the proportion: p̂ ± Z·√[p̂(1−p̂)/n]. A 95% CI does NOT mean a 95% probability that this particular interval contains μ — μ is fixed; the interval is random. To shrink a CI: increase n, decrease confidence level or reduce variability. Required sample size: for the mean n = (Zσ/E)²; for the proportion n = Z²p(1−p)/E².

Key Concepts

  • Point estimate vs interval estimate
  • Confidence level (1 − α) and significance level α
  • Critical values Z_α/2, t_α/2 (df = n − 1)
  • Margin of error E = critical × SE
  • Wider CI ⇔ higher confidence, smaller n or higher variability
  • Sample size for mean and proportion

Quiz Practice Quiz

Q1. A 95% CI means:
  • 95% probability μ lies in THIS interval
  • In repeated sampling, 95% of such intervals contain μ
  • Sample mean equals μ with 95% probability
  • There is a 5% chance the sample is wrong
Explanation: Confidence refers to the long-run capture rate, not a probability about the parameter.
Q2. When σ is unknown and n is small, the CI for μ uses:
  • Z-distribution
  • t-distribution
  • Chi-square
  • F-distribution
Explanation: We estimate σ by s and use Student's t with n − 1 df.
Q3. To halve the margin of error (same confidence), n must:
  • Double
  • Quadruple
  • Halve
  • Stay the same
Explanation: E ∝ 1/√n; halving E needs n × 4.
Q4. Critical Z for a 99% CI is approximately:
  • 1.28
  • 1.645
  • 1.96
  • 2.576
Explanation: α/2 = 0.005 → Z = 2.576.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Define a confidence interval.
An interval estimate computed from sample data designed to contain the true parameter with a specified long-run frequency (e.g. 95% of all such intervals contain μ).
When should you use the t-distribution instead of Z for the mean?
When the population standard deviation σ is unknown and we estimate it with s (small samples); always safe when n < 30 and the population is roughly normal.
Give the sample-size formula for estimating a proportion within margin E.
n = Z²·p(1−p)/E². Use p = 0.5 when no prior estimate is available (most conservative).
How do you narrow a CI without losing precision?
Increase the sample size; reducing confidence also narrows the interval but at the cost of higher Type I-style risk.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

A confidence interval converts a point estimate into a range that explicitly communicates sampling uncertainty. It is built from three ingredients: a point estimate (e.g. x̄ for μ or p̂ for p), a critical value from the appropriate distribution (Z when σ is known or for large-sample proportions; t with n − 1 degrees of freedom for the mean when σ is unknown), and a standard error. For the mean the CI is x̄ ± t_α/2 · s/√n; for a proportion it is p̂ ± Z_α/2 · √[p̂(1−p̂)/n]. The confidence level (1 − α), commonly 90%, 95% or 99%, refers to long-run frequency: if we repeated the sampling procedure many times, that proportion of the resulting intervals would capture the true parameter. It does not assign probability to the parameter, which is fixed. The margin of error E = critical × SE quantifies the half-width; managers can shrink E by increasing the sample size (n ∝ 1/E²), lowering confidence or reducing variability through stratification. Required sample size for the mean is n = (Zσ/E)²; for proportions n = Z²p(1 − p)/E², with p = 0.5 yielding the most conservative answer. Confidence intervals are therefore richer than single-number estimates because they expose both the best guess and the precision of that guess, which is precisely what good decisions require.
