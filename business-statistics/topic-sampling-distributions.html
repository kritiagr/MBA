Sampling Distributions

In Simple Language

A sampling distribution is the probability distribution of a statistic (like the sample mean x̄ or proportion p̂) over all possible samples of size n. For the mean, E(x̄) = μ and SE = σ/√n. By the Central Limit Theorem, x̄ is approximately normal for n ≥ 30 even when the population is not normal. For the proportion, E(p̂) = p and SE = √[p(1−p)/n], approximately normal when np ≥ 5 and n(1−p) ≥ 5. Sampling distributions explain why inference works — they tell us how far a sample statistic is likely to be from the true parameter.

Key Concepts

  • Statistic vs parameter; sampling distribution
  • E(x̄) = μ; standard error SE = σ/√n
  • Central Limit Theorem (CLT) — n ≥ 30 rule of thumb
  • Sampling distribution of the proportion: SE = √[p(1−p)/n]
  • Finite population correction (when n/N > 0.05)
  • Standard error decreases with √n

Q1. Standard error of the mean is:
  • σ
  • σ²/n
  • σ/√n
  • σ/n
Explanation: SE(x̄) = σ/√n; halving SE needs a fourfold increase in n.
Q2. CLT requires n to be at least approximately:
  • 5
  • 10
  • 30
  • 100
Explanation: n ≥ 30 is the common rule of thumb for x̄ to be approximately normal.
Q3. For p̂ to be approximately normal, we need:
  • n ≥ 10
  • np ≥ 5 and n(1-p) ≥ 5
  • p = 0.5
  • n = 100
Explanation: Both np and n(1−p) should be at least 5 for normality.
Q4. If sample size quadruples, SE of the mean:
  • Doubles
  • Halves
  • Quadruples
  • Quarters
Explanation: SE ∝ 1/√n; n × 4 ⇒ SE / 2.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Define standard error.
The standard deviation of a sampling distribution; for the sample mean it is σ/√n and measures the typical distance between x̄ and μ.
State the Central Limit Theorem.
For sufficiently large n (≥ 30), the sampling distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal with mean μ and standard error σ/√n, regardless of the parent distribution shape.
Why is the CLT important?
It lets us use normal-based methods (Z-tests, confidence intervals) for the mean even when the underlying population is not normal.
When does the finite-population correction apply?
When the sample is more than 5% of the population (n/N > 0.05); multiply SE by √[(N − n)/(N − 1)].

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

A sampling distribution is the probability distribution of a sample statistic — such as the sample mean x̄ or sample proportion p̂ — calculated over every possible sample of a given size from the population. Understanding sampling distributions is what allows us to make trustworthy inferences from a single sample. For the sample mean we have two foundational results: the expected value E(x̄) equals the population mean μ, so x̄ is an unbiased estimator; and the standard error SE = σ/√n quantifies how much x̄ varies from sample to sample. Crucially, the Central Limit Theorem says that for a sufficiently large sample (often n ≥ 30), the distribution of x̄ is approximately normal even if the population itself is not, which is why so many downstream inference procedures rely on the normal distribution. For proportions, E(p̂) = p with SE = √[p(1−p)/n]; the sampling distribution is approximately normal when np ≥ 5 and n(1 − p) ≥ 5. Because SE shrinks with √n, larger samples produce more precise estimates — but halving SE requires quadrupling n. When the sample is a sizeable fraction of a finite population (n/N > 0.05) we apply a finite-population correction factor. Sampling distributions thus form the bridge between a single study's data and the broader population conclusions we wish to draw.
