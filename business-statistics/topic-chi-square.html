Chi-Square Tests

In Simple Language

Chi-square tests use the χ² distribution to compare observed and expected frequencies for categorical data. Three flavours: (1) Goodness-of-fit — does an observed frequency distribution match a hypothesised one? (2) Independence — are two categorical variables in a contingency table independent? (3) Homogeneity — do several populations share the same distribution across categories? Statistic: χ² = Σ (O − E)² / E. Expected count for cell (i, j) in independence = (row total × column total) / grand total. df = (rows − 1)(cols − 1) for independence; (k − 1) for goodness-of-fit. Reject H₀ when χ² exceeds the critical value or p < α. Requires all expected counts ≥ 5.

Key Concepts

  • χ² goodness-of-fit test (one variable vs theoretical distribution)
  • χ² test of independence (two categorical variables)
  • χ² test of homogeneity (same distribution across populations)
  • Expected frequency = (row total × column total) / grand total
  • Degrees of freedom rules
  • Assumption: expected counts ≥ 5 in every cell

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

State the chi-square test statistic.
χ² = Σ (Oᵢ − Eᵢ)² / Eᵢ, where Oᵢ are observed and Eᵢ are expected frequencies.
Difference between independence and homogeneity tests.
Independence: one sample, two variables — are they associated? Homogeneity: several samples (one per population) measured on one variable — are the distributions the same?
How are expected counts computed in a contingency table?
E_ij = (Row total × Column total) / Grand total, under H₀ of independence.
What if some expected counts are below 5?
Combine adjacent categories or use Fisher's exact test, because the χ² approximation can be unreliable.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Chi-square (χ²) tests are the standard tools for analysing categorical data. The test statistic χ² = Σ (O − E)² / E sums standardised squared differences between observed counts O and counts E expected under the null hypothesis. Three closely related procedures dominate practice. A goodness-of-fit test asks whether the observed frequency distribution of a single categorical variable matches a hypothesised distribution — for example, whether customer preferences across four brands match the company's market-share assumptions; degrees of freedom equal k − 1 (minus extra estimated parameters). A test of independence asks whether two categorical variables observed jointly in a contingency table are statistically associated — for example, whether smoking status is related to gender; expected counts under independence are (row total × column total) / grand total, and df = (r − 1)(c − 1). A test of homogeneity compares the same categorical variable across several independent populations, again using a contingency table. In every case we reject H₀ when χ² exceeds the critical value from the χ² distribution or, equivalently, when p < α. The key assumption is that expected counts in every cell are at least 5; otherwise we should combine categories or switch to Fisher's exact test. Chi-square is invaluable for survey analysis, market research, quality inspection and any setting where evidence comes as counts in categories.
