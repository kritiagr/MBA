Simple Linear Regression

In Simple Language

Simple linear regression fits a straight line ŷ = b₀ + b₁x to model how one dependent variable y depends on one independent variable x. The slope b₁ is the expected change in y for a one-unit change in x; the intercept b₀ is the predicted y when x = 0. Coefficients are estimated by ordinary least squares (OLS), which minimises the sum of squared residuals. The coefficient of determination R² (= SSR/SST) is the proportion of variation in y explained by x; the standard error of the estimate s_yx measures typical prediction error. Significance of the slope is tested using t = b₁ / SE(b₁). Assumptions: linearity, independence, normality and constant variance of residuals (LINE).

Key Concepts

  • Scatter plot, covariance, correlation r (−1 to +1)
  • Least-squares line ŷ = b₀ + b₁x; b₁ = r·(s_y/s_x); b₀ = ȳ − b₁x̄
  • Sum of squares: SST = SSR + SSE; R² = SSR/SST
  • Standard error of the estimate s_yx
  • Inference: t-test for slope; confidence interval for slope
  • Assumptions (LINE): Linearity, Independence, Normality, Equal variance

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Interpret the slope b₁ in business language.
For every unit increase in x, y is predicted to change by b₁ units, holding other factors constant; the sign indicates direction.
What does R² measure?
The proportion of total variation in the dependent variable explained by the regression line; ranges 0–1, higher meaning better fit.
List the four key assumptions of simple linear regression.
LINE — Linearity of the relationship, Independence of residuals, Normality of residuals, and Equal variance (homoscedasticity).
Difference between correlation and regression.
Correlation (r) measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables; regression provides a predictive equation and quantifies how much y changes per unit x.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Simple linear regression models the relationship between a single dependent variable y and a single independent variable x as a straight line ŷ = b₀ + b₁x. The intercept b₀ is the predicted value of y when x = 0; the slope b₁ tells us how much y is expected to change for each one-unit increase in x. Coefficients are estimated by ordinary least squares, which chooses b₀ and b₁ to minimise the sum of squared residuals SSE = Σ(y − ŷ)². The total variability in y, SST = Σ(y − ȳ)², is decomposed as SST = SSR + SSE, where SSR is the variability explained by the regression. The coefficient of determination R² = SSR/SST measures the fraction of variability explained; the standard error of the estimate s_yx measures typical prediction error. Significance of the linear relationship is tested via t = b₁/SE(b₁) with n − 2 degrees of freedom; the corresponding confidence interval expresses the slope's uncertainty. Four classical assumptions must be checked — Linearity, Independence of residuals, Normality of residuals and Equal variance (LINE) — using residual plots and normal probability plots. Once validated, regression supports both estimation (mean response confidence interval at a given x) and prediction (wider interval for a new individual observation). The technique underpins sales forecasting, demand modelling, pricing analysis and almost all data-driven business decisions involving a quantitative driver and outcome.
