The Normal Distribution

In Simple Language

The normal (Gaussian) distribution is a continuous, bell-shaped distribution defined by its mean μ and standard deviation σ. It is symmetric around μ; mean = median = mode. About 68% of values lie within ±1σ, 95% within ±2σ, 99.7% within ±3σ (empirical rule). To find probabilities we standardise to Z = (X − μ)/σ and use the standard normal table. Normality matters because the Central Limit Theorem makes sample means approximately normal even when raw data is not, justifying most statistical inference.

Key Concepts

  • Properties of normal: symmetric, bell-shaped, μ = median = mode
  • Standard normal Z = (X − μ) / σ
  • Z-tables (cumulative probabilities)
  • Empirical Rule 68-95-99.7
  • Inverse problem: find X given probability — X = μ + Zσ
  • Why normality matters: CLT and downstream tests

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Define the standard normal distribution.
A normal distribution with mean 0 and standard deviation 1, denoted Z; used to compute probabilities for any normal variable after standardisation.
How do you find P(X < x) for a normal variable?
Compute Z = (x − μ)/σ, then look up Φ(Z) in the standard normal table to get the cumulative probability.
State the Empirical Rule.
For approximately normal data, about 68% of values fall within ±1σ of the mean, 95% within ±2σ, and 99.7% within ±3σ.
Why is the normal distribution central to statistics?
Many natural and economic variables are approximately normal, and by the Central Limit Theorem, sample means tend to a normal distribution, enabling inference.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

The normal distribution is the most important continuous distribution in statistics. It is a symmetric, bell-shaped curve fully described by two parameters — its mean μ, which locates the centre, and its standard deviation σ, which controls the spread. Mean, median and mode all coincide at μ. The empirical rule says that approximately 68% of observations lie within one standard deviation of the mean, 95% within two, and 99.7% within three; this is the source of the ubiquitous 'six sigma' quality concept. To compute probabilities for any normal variable we standardise: Z = (X − μ)/σ converts the value into a standard normal score whose cumulative probability Φ(Z) is read from a table. The inverse problem — find X such that P(X ≤ x) = p — uses X = μ + Z_p·σ. The normal distribution is so important because (a) many naturally occurring measurements such as heights, exam scores and process errors are approximately normal, (b) the Central Limit Theorem ensures that sample means and proportions are approximately normal regardless of the parent distribution for sufficiently large samples, and (c) it underpins confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, regression and control charts. Mastery of the normal therefore unlocks almost the entire inferential toolkit.
