Organising and Visualising Variables

In Simple Language

Raw data is rarely useful on its own. Organising techniques turn lists of values into summaries: frequency, relative-frequency and cumulative distributions for one variable; contingency tables for two categorical variables. Visualisation then makes patterns obvious. Categorical: bar chart, pie chart, Pareto chart, side-by-side bar. Numerical: histogram, polygon, ogive, stem-and-leaf, dot plot, box plot. Two numerical variables: scatter plot, time-series plot. Good charts have clear titles, labelled axes, a sensible scale and no chart-junk.

Key Concepts

  • Frequency / relative-frequency / cumulative distribution
  • Contingency table (cross-tab) for two categorical variables
  • Bar, pie, Pareto charts; side-by-side bars

  • Scatter plot, time-series plot

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Difference between bar chart and histogram.
Bar charts display categorical data with gaps between bars. Histograms display continuous numerical data with adjacent bars showing class frequencies.
What is a Pareto chart?
A bar chart of categorical data sorted in descending order of frequency with a cumulative percentage line — used to identify the 'vital few' contributors to a problem.
Why is a pie chart often a weak choice?
Humans judge angles poorly, so small slices look similar; a bar chart usually communicates the same data more accurately.
Name three elements of a well-designed chart.
Clear title, labelled axes with units, and a sensible scale starting (usually) at zero with no misleading distortion.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Organising and visualising data converts raw observations into information a manager can act on. For a single categorical variable we tabulate frequencies and relative frequencies and display them with bar, pie or Pareto charts; for two categoricals we use a contingency table (cross-tab) and side-by-side or stacked bar charts to compare groups. For a single numerical variable we build a frequency distribution by grouping values into classes, then visualise the shape with a histogram, polygon, ogive, stem-and-leaf display, dot plot or box plot — each highlighting a different facet (distribution shape, cumulative behaviour, raw values, central tendency and outliers). For two numerical variables a scatter plot reveals direction, form and strength of association; a time-series plot shows trend and seasonality. Good practice means a clear title, labelled axes with units, a fair scale that does not distort, minimal chart-junk and consistent colour. Beware misleading techniques such as truncated y-axes, 3D effects, inconsistent bin widths or selective scaling. Organising and visualisation are not decorative steps — they expose outliers, skewness, gaps and relationships that drive every downstream analysis, making them the first and arguably most consequential decisions in any statistical project.
