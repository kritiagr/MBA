Two-Sample Tests and ANOVA

In Simple Language

Two-sample tests compare parameters of two populations. Independent means: pooled or Welch's t-test on x̄₁ − x̄₂. Related (paired) means: paired t-test on the differences. Two proportions: Z-test using pooled p̂. Two variances: F-test on s₁²/s₂². ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) extends this to compare three or more group means simultaneously without inflating the Type I error rate. One-way ANOVA partitions total variability into between-group (SSB) and within-group (SSW) components, and tests H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = ... = μ_k using F = MSB/MSW. A significant F tells us at least one mean differs — post-hoc tests like Tukey identify which pairs.

Key Concepts

  • Independent vs related-samples tests
  • Pooled-variance vs separate-variance (Welch's) t-test
  • Paired t-test (matched pairs)
  • Z-test for two proportions
  • F-test for two variances
  • One-way ANOVA: SST = SSB + SSW; F = MSB/MSW; df_between = k − 1, df_within = N − k
  • Tukey or Bonferroni post-hoc comparisons

  • Variation between group means
  • Variation inside each group
  • Total variation
  • Difference of variances
Explanation: SSW = unexplained or 'error' variation around each group mean.
Q4. A significant F in one-way ANOVA implies:
  • All means are equal
  • All means differ
  • At least one pair of means differs

Explanation: F-rejection tells us some difference exists; post-hoc identifies which pairs.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

When should you use a paired t-test?
When the two samples are naturally matched — e.g. before-and-after measurements on the same subjects — so we analyse the within-pair differences.
State the assumptions of one-way ANOVA.
Independent random samples from each group, approximately normal populations and (approximately) equal group variances (homoscedasticity).
Why use ANOVA instead of multiple t-tests?
Running many pairwise t-tests inflates the overall Type I error; ANOVA controls α at the chosen level for a single global test.
Difference between SSB and SSW.
SSB measures variation between group means (explained); SSW measures variation within groups (residual). F = (SSB/df₁) / (SSW/df₂).

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Two-sample tests and analysis of variance let managers compare groups under uncertainty. When comparing the means of two independent samples, we use an independent-samples t-test — pooled when variances are equal, Welch's version when they are not. When the samples are naturally paired (before/after, twins, matched subjects), the paired t-test operates on the within-pair differences and removes between-subject noise. To compare two proportions we use a Z-test that pools the successes; to compare two variances we use an F-test on the ratio s₁²/s₂². When we need to compare three or more groups simultaneously, running multiple t-tests would inflate the Type I error, so we use Analysis of Variance (ANOVA). One-way ANOVA partitions the total sum of squares into a between-group component (SSB), which captures variation attributable to the grouping factor, and a within-group component (SSW), which represents residual error. The F-statistic, MSB / MSW with k − 1 and N − k degrees of freedom, compares explained to unexplained variation. A significant F indicates that at least one group mean differs, but does not say which — post-hoc procedures such as Tukey's HSD or Bonferroni-corrected pairwise comparisons identify the specific differences while controlling the family-wise error rate. Assumptions of independence, normality and equal variances should always be checked before interpretation.
