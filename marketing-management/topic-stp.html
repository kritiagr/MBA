Segmentation, Targeting and Positioning (STP)

In Simple Language

STP is the strategic heart of marketing. Segmentation divides a heterogeneous market into smaller, more homogeneous groups. Bases: Geographic (region, city size, climate), Demographic (age, gender, income, occupation, family life cycle), Psychographic (lifestyle, values, personality), Behavioural (occasion, benefits sought, usage rate, loyalty status). Good segments are Measurable, Accessible, Substantial, Differentiable, Actionable (MASDA). Targeting evaluates and selects segments — strategies include Undifferentiated (mass), Differentiated (multiple segments, different mixes), Concentrated (niche) and Micromarketing. Positioning is the act of designing the company's offering and image to occupy a distinctive place in the target customer's mind. Positioning statement: 'To [target segment], [brand] is the [frame of reference] that [point of difference], because [reason to believe].' Tools: Perceptual maps, points of parity vs points of difference.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

List the four bases of market segmentation.
Geographic (region, city, climate), Demographic (age, gender, income, occupation), Psychographic (lifestyle, values, personality), and Behavioural (occasion, benefits, usage rate, loyalty).
State the MASDA criteria.
Measurable — size and purchasing power can be quantified; Accessible — segment can be reached and served; Substantial — large/profitable enough to serve; Differentiable — responds differently to different mixes; Actionable — effective programmes can be designed.
Define positioning.
Designing the company's offering and image to occupy a distinctive, valued place in the target customer's mind — creating an associative perception of what the brand stands for relative to competitors.
Differentiate POP and POD.
Points of Parity (POP) are attributes a brand must have to be a credible competitor in a category (e.g. coffee must be hot). Points of Difference (POD) are attributes that distinguish and differentiate the brand and provide reasons to choose it (e.g. Starbucks' premium experience).

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Segmentation, Targeting and Positioning — collectively STP — is the strategic core of modern marketing, the bridge from market analysis to the tactical 4Ps. Segmentation begins with the premise that markets are heterogeneous and divides them into smaller, more homogeneous groups whose members share similar needs and respond similarly to marketing stimuli. The four classical bases are Geographic (country, region, urban/rural, climate), Demographic (age, gender, income, education, family life cycle, religion — the most popular because data is readily available), Psychographic (lifestyle, values, personality, AIOs — Activities, Interests, Opinions) and Behavioural (occasion of use, benefits sought, user status, usage rate, loyalty status, buyer-readiness stage). A useful segmentation must satisfy the MASDA criteria — Measurable, Accessible, Substantial, Differentiable and Actionable. Targeting evaluates each segment for attractiveness (size, growth, profitability, competitive intensity, fit with company resources) and selects one or more segments to serve. Four broad strategies exist: Undifferentiated (mass) marketing offers one product to the whole market — fast but vulnerable; Differentiated marketing creates different mixes for several segments — broader reach but costlier; Concentrated (niche) marketing focuses deeply on one small segment — high relevance, vulnerable to demand shifts; Micromarketing tailors offerings to individuals or local areas. Positioning, the final stage, is the act of designing the company's offering and image so that it occupies a distinctive and desired place in the target customer's mind relative to competitors. A robust positioning statement names the target segment, the frame of reference (the category), the point of difference, and the reason to believe — e.g. 'To young urban professionals, Volvo is the car that delivers the safest driving experience because of its industry-leading engineering and crash-testing.' Marketers use perceptual maps to visualise their position on two or more relevant attributes, and consciously craft Points of Parity (must-haves to be in the category) and Points of Difference (reasons to choose this brand). When markets evolve, brands engage in repositioning — Tata Tea moved from a commodity to a 'Jaago Re' social-awakening positioning, for instance.
