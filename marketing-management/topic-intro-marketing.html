Definition and Scope of Marketing

In Simple Language

Marketing is the process by which firms create value for customers and build strong customer relationships in order to capture value in return. AMA definition: 'the activity, set of institutions and processes for creating, communicating, delivering and exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners and society at large.' Core concepts: Needs (basic requirements — food, security), Wants (specific form of need shaped by culture and personality — biryani vs pasta), Demand (wants backed by purchasing power). Marketing evolved through five orientations: Production, Product, Selling, Marketing, and Societal Marketing. The Marketing Mix (McCarthy's 4Ps — Product, Price, Place, Promotion) is the tactical toolkit; services add 3 more (People, Process, Physical Evidence = 7Ps). Marketing creates form, time, place, and possession utility.

Key Concepts

  • Definitions of marketing (AMA, Kotler)
  • Needs vs Wants vs Demands
  • Five marketing orientations: Production, Product, Selling, Marketing, Societal

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

Differentiate needs, wants and demands.
Needs are basic human requirements (food, shelter, safety). Wants are specific forms of needs shaped by culture and individuality (a need for food becomes a want for biryani). Demands are wants backed by purchasing power and willingness to buy.
List the four marketing mix elements (4Ps).
Product (offering and benefits), Price (what customer pays), Place (distribution and channels), Promotion (communication: ads, sales promotion, PR, personal selling).
Compare Selling and Marketing concepts.
Selling starts with the factory, focuses on existing products and uses aggressive promotion to push sales — emphasis on the seller's needs. Marketing starts with the customer, focuses on identifying needs and producing integrated marketing to satisfy them profitably — emphasis on the buyer's needs.
Define Societal Marketing.
An orientation in which the company's task is to determine the needs and wants of target markets and deliver desired satisfactions more effectively than competitors in a way that preserves or enhances the consumer's and society's well-being (e.g. green packaging, fair sourcing).

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Marketing is the comprehensive process by which organizations create value for customers and build profitable, lasting customer relationships in order to capture value in return. The American Marketing Association defines it as the activity, set of institutions and processes for creating, communicating, delivering and exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners and society at large. Three core concepts anchor the field. Needs are the basic states of felt deprivation shared by all humans — food, shelter, safety, belonging. Wants are the specific forms that needs take when shaped by culture, personality and context — the same hunger that produces a want for noodles in Tokyo produces a want for chole bhature in Delhi. Demands are wants backed by purchasing power and the willingness to buy. Marketing thinking has evolved through five orientations. The Production concept (1900s) assumes consumers prefer widely available, low-cost products; the Product concept assumes they favour the highest quality and features; the Selling concept (1930s–40s) assumes consumers will not buy enough unless aggressively pushed; the Marketing concept (post-1950s) starts with customer needs and integrates the organization to meet them profitably; the Societal Marketing concept (1970s onwards) extends this by also serving society's long-term welfare. The Marketing Mix, popularised as McCarthy's 4Ps, is the tactical toolkit — Product, Price, Place and Promotion — that managers blend to deliver the firm's value proposition. Service businesses extend this to 7Ps by adding People, Process and Physical Evidence, recognising that intangibility, inseparability and variability matter. Marketing ultimately creates four kinds of utility — form (transforming inputs into desired products), time (making them available when needed), place (making them available where needed) and possession (transferring ownership and after-sales support) — and is therefore central to value creation in any economy.
