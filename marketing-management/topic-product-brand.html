Product & Brand Management

In Simple Language

A product is anything offered to a market for attention, acquisition, use or consumption that satisfies a want or need. Five product levels (Kotler): Core benefit, Basic product, Expected product, Augmented product, Potential product. Product classifications: Consumer (convenience, shopping, specialty, unsought) and Industrial (materials/parts, capital items, supplies/services). Product Mix: width (lines), length (items), depth (variants), consistency. Product Life Cycle (PLC): Introduction, Growth, Maturity, Decline — with different marketing-mix strategies at each stage. New Product Development (NPD) stages: Idea Generation → Idea Screening → Concept Development & Testing → Marketing Strategy → Business Analysis → Product Development → Test Marketing → Commercialisation. A Brand is a name, term, sign, symbol or design that identifies a maker's product and differentiates it. Brand equity (Aaker; Keller's CBBE) — the value premium customers attach to a brand — is built through awareness, perceived quality, associations and loyalty. Strategies: Brand extension, Line extension, Multi-brand, Co-branding, Private labels.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

List Kotler's five product levels.
Core Benefit (the fundamental need served), Basic Product (basic version), Expected Product (set of attributes buyers normally expect), Augmented Product (additional services/benefits — warranty, delivery), Potential Product (all future augmentations/transformations the offering may undergo).
Name the four stages of the Product Life Cycle.
Introduction (slow sales, high cost, losses), Growth (rapid acceptance, profits rise), Maturity (slowdown in growth, peak profits, competition intense), Decline (sales fall, products withdrawn).
Differentiate line extension and brand extension.
Line Extension: launching new variants in the same product category under the same brand name (Coke Zero, Diet Coke). Brand Extension: using the existing brand name to enter a new product category (Tata Salt → Tata Tea → Tata Motors).
State Keller's CBBE pyramid steps.
Brand Identity (salience/awareness), Brand Meaning (performance and imagery), Brand Response (judgements and feelings), and Brand Relationships (resonance/loyalty).

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

A product is anything that can be offered to a market for attention, acquisition, use or consumption that satisfies a need or want. Kotler's five product levels deepen the analysis: the Core Benefit is the fundamental need the customer is really buying (a hotel sells rest); the Basic Product converts that into a tangible offering (a room with a bed); the Expected Product is the set of attributes buyers normally expect (clean linen, working AC); the Augmented Product adds extras that exceed expectations (free Wi-Fi, late checkout, complimentary breakfast); the Potential Product encompasses all future augmentations the offering may undergo. Products are classified for marketing decisions: consumer goods break into convenience (frequent, low effort — soap), shopping (compared on price/quality — clothes), specialty (unique characteristics, brand loyalty — luxury cars) and unsought (insurance, funeral services); industrial goods include materials and parts, capital items, and supplies and services. The Product Mix is described by width (number of product lines), length (total items), depth (variants per item) and consistency (how related the lines are). The Product Life Cycle traces a typical S-curve through Introduction (slow growth, heavy losses, focus on awareness), Growth (rapid acceptance, profits accelerate, focus on market share), Maturity (slowdown, peak profits, competitive pressure, focus on differentiation and segment-targeting) and Decline (falling sales, focus on harvesting or divestment). New Product Development moves systematically from Idea Generation through Idea Screening, Concept Development and Testing, Marketing Strategy, Business Analysis, Product Development, Test Marketing and finally Commercialisation. A Brand is the name, symbol or design that identifies an offering and differentiates it — and ultimately the bundle of associations in the customer's mind. Brand equity is the value premium that a recognisable brand earns. David Aaker frames equity as awareness, perceived quality, associations and loyalty; Kevin Keller's Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) pyramid ascends from Salience → Performance and Imagery → Judgements and Feelings → Resonance/Loyalty. Strategy choices include line extensions (Coke Zero), brand extensions (Tata Motors), multi-brand (P&G's many shampoos) and co-branding (Tata-Starbucks). Packaging and labelling — the silent salesmen — protect the product, communicate the brand, comply with regulation and increasingly bear sustainability responsibilities.
