Market Research & Industrial Marketing

In Simple Language

Marketing Research is the systematic design, collection, analysis and reporting of data and findings relevant to a specific marketing situation. Process: Define problem → Develop research plan → Collect information → Analyse → Present findings → Make decision. Sources: Primary (surveys, observation, interviews, focus groups, experiments) and Secondary (existing — internal records, industry reports, government, syndicated like Nielsen). Methods: Quantitative (surveys, conjoint analysis) vs Qualitative (focus groups, in-depth interviews, ethnography). Industrial / B2B Marketing differs from B2C: derived demand, fewer larger buyers, professional purchasing, multiple decision-makers (Buying Centre — initiator, user, influencer, decider, approver, buyer, gatekeeper), longer cycles, focus on relationships, technical specifications and total cost of ownership. Buying-situation types (Robinson): New Task, Modified Rebuy, Straight Rebuy.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

List the six steps of the marketing research process.
1. Define the problem and research objectives. 2. Develop the research plan. 3. Collect the information. 4. Analyse the information. 5. Present the findings. 6. Make the decision.
Differentiate primary and secondary data.
Primary data are original data collected specifically for the current research problem (surveys, interviews, experiments, observation). Secondary data are existing data collected earlier for other purposes (internal records, industry reports, government statistics, syndicated research).
How does B2B marketing differ from B2C?
B2B has fewer, larger, professional buyers; derived demand; more concentrated geographic markets; multiple decision-makers in a Buying Centre; longer sales cycles; emphasis on technical specifications, customisation and total cost of ownership; and deep, long-term relationships with key accounts.
Name the three buying situations in industrial markets.
New Task (first-time purchase, lengthy evaluation), Modified Rebuy (some changes from past purchase — specifications, suppliers), and Straight Rebuy (routine reorder of same item from same supplier).

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Marketing Research is the systematic design, collection, analysis and reporting of data and findings relevant to a specific marketing situation facing the company — its goal is to reduce uncertainty in decisions about segmentation, targeting, positioning, product, price, place and promotion. A disciplined research process moves through six steps: define the problem and research objectives, develop the research plan (sources, approach, instruments, sampling plan, contact methods), collect information, analyse it, present findings and make decisions. Data sources are primary — original data gathered for the problem at hand via surveys, observation, in-depth interviews, focus groups, experiments and increasingly digital behavioural data — or secondary, drawing on existing sources such as internal records, government statistics, industry reports and syndicated research from firms like Nielsen, Kantar and IMRB. Quantitative methods (large-sample surveys, conjoint analysis, A/B tests) measure what and how much; qualitative methods (focus groups, ethnography, in-depth interviews) explain why. Robust research depends on careful sampling (probability vs non-probability), well-designed questionnaires, validity (we measure what we intend) and reliability (consistent results). Industrial or B2B marketing differs from B2C in several ways: demand is derived from end-consumer demand and prone to the accelerator effect; buyers are fewer and larger; purchasing is professional; markets are geographically concentrated; and decisions are made by a Buying Centre with multiple roles — Initiator (recognises need), User (uses the product), Influencer (shapes specs, often technical), Decider (final authority), Approver (sanctions), Buyer (negotiates and orders) and Gatekeeper (controls information flow). Robinson's framework identifies three buying situations: a New Task (first-time, lengthy, all phases of decision), a Modified Rebuy (some changes — opportunity for new entrants) and a Straight Rebuy (routine, low involvement — incumbent advantage). Industrial marketers compete on technical specifications, customisation, delivery reliability, after-sales service and total cost of ownership rather than mass advertising. Strategies emphasise key account management, relationship marketing, trade shows, technical seminars, white papers and increasingly digital tools such as LinkedIn marketing and account-based marketing (ABM). Together, rigorous research and B2B marketing competence enable companies to serve complex organizational buyers as thoughtfully as they serve households.
