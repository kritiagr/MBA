Channels of Distribution & Supply Chain

In Simple Language

Marketing channels are sets of interdependent organizations that help make a product or service available for consumption. They create five key flows: physical, ownership, payment, information and promotion. Functions: information gathering, promotion, contact, matching, negotiation, physical distribution, financing, risk-taking. Channel levels: Zero-level (direct — Dell), One-level (one intermediary — retailer), Two-level (wholesaler + retailer), Three-level (agent, wholesaler, retailer). Channel design balances customer service needs with channel objectives. Channel-coverage strategies: Intensive (FMCG — soap, biscuits), Selective (consumer durables), Exclusive (luxury cars, designer brands). Vertical Marketing Systems (VMS): Corporate, Contractual (franchising — McDonald's), Administered (Walmart's leadership over its suppliers). Horizontal Marketing Systems pool resources across companies at the same level. Conflicts: horizontal (among same-level intermediaries), vertical (between channel levels), multi-channel (showrooming, channel-cannibalisation). Modern: e-commerce, omnichannel and direct-to-consumer.

Short Answers Short-Answer Q&A

List the three distribution intensity strategies with one example each.
Intensive: maximum outlets — convenience goods like Parle-G biscuits. Selective: a chosen few outlets — consumer durables like LG TVs. Exclusive: very few outlets in a territory — luxury brands like Rolex or Audi.
What is a Vertical Marketing System (VMS)?
A channel structure in which producer, wholesaler and retailer act as a unified system. Three types: Corporate (one firm owns multiple levels — Reliance Retail), Contractual (independent firms linked by contracts — franchises) and Administered (informal leadership by a dominant member — Walmart).
Differentiate horizontal and vertical channel conflict.
Horizontal conflict occurs between firms at the same channel level (e.g. two dealers fighting over territory). Vertical conflict occurs between different channel levels (e.g. a manufacturer cutting prices online, upsetting offline retailers).
Why do producers use intermediaries despite the cost?
Intermediaries provide contacts, experience, specialisation and scale economies — they perform channel functions more efficiently than the producer could alone, and reduce the number of contacts needed to reach the end customer.

Long Answer 150-Word Answer

Marketing channels — also called distribution channels — are sets of interdependent organizations involved in making a product or service available for use or consumption. They generate five flows that move between producer and consumer: the physical flow of goods, the ownership/title flow, the payment flow, the information flow (market intelligence and feedback) and the promotion flow. Together they perform a range of functions including information gathering, promotion, contact with prospects, matching the offer to buyer needs, negotiation, physical distribution, financing, and risk-taking. Channels vary in length — described by the number of intermediary levels. A zero-level (direct) channel sells producer-to-consumer (Amul's milk parlours, Dell historically); a one-level channel adds a retailer; a two-level channel adds a wholesaler before the retailer; a three-level channel adds an agent. Channel design matches customer service requirements (lot size, waiting time, spatial convenience, product variety, service backup) with channel objectives and constraints. Distribution intensity is then chosen: Intensive distribution puts the product in as many outlets as possible — appropriate for convenience goods like soap and biscuits; Selective distribution uses a chosen few outlets — typical for shopping goods like consumer durables; Exclusive distribution limits outlets sharply — fitting for luxury and specialty goods that depend on image and personal service. Vertical Marketing Systems (VMS) coordinate the channel as a unified network: Corporate VMS (one firm owns successive stages — Reliance Industries to Reliance Retail), Contractual VMS (independent firms bound by contracts, including franchising — McDonald's, KFC) and Administered VMS (leadership by a dominant member — Walmart over its suppliers). Horizontal Marketing Systems pool resources across same-level firms; multi-channel systems use several channels simultaneously. Channel conflicts inevitably arise — horizontal among same-level members, vertical between levels (manufacturer vs retailer over price or e-commerce), and multi-channel friction such as showrooming. Modern distribution has been transformed by e-commerce, omnichannel experiences and direct-to-consumer (D2C) brands like Boat, mamaearth and Nykaa, which use digital channels to bypass traditional layers, build first-party customer data and improve margins. Underlying all this is logistics and Supply Chain Management — moving the right product to the right place at the right time at the lowest total cost — increasingly powered by data, AI and sustainability considerations.
